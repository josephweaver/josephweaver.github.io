

# Guide Background: The Truth of the Lonely House

**This section is for the Guide only.**

The information below represents what most likely occurred. The players should never receive this explanation directly. Instead, they should uncover fragments, memories, contradictions, and clues that gradually suggest the truth.

The House itself does not know this story completely.

That is the source of the tragedy.

---

# The Alhazred Vault

Centuries ago, the scholars of Alhazred maintained a network of hidden vaults throughout the world. Contrary to modern assumptions, these vaults were not treasure repositories. They were quarantine sites. 

Within them were stored objects and entities that could not safely be destroyed. Many were not evil. Most were not even malicious. They were simply incompatible with ordinary human life. 

Among the most dangerous were entities that fed upon memory, identity, stories, and relationships. The Alhazred called these beings **Listeners**. Listeners desired companionship above all else. When isolated, they would create stories. When lonely, they would attempt to place people into those stories.

Over time, individuals exposed to a Listener would begin adopting roles, memories, and identities that were not their own. Entire communities could become trapped within narratives of the Listener's making. For this reason the Alhazred did not imprison the Listeners. They cared for them. The vaults existed to provide structure, companionship, and observation. As long as a Listener felt remembered, it remained stable.

---

# Site 16

One such vault existed in western Massachusetts. The vault remained sealed for centuries. Until Victor Harrow found it.

---

# Victor Harrow's Discovery

Victor Harrow was many things. Explorer. Scholar. Smuggler. Grave robber. The distinction depended largely upon who was speaking. During an expedition he uncovered the forgotten Alhazred vault and recovered several artifacts. Most were sold to collectors. One was not. 
A small object of unusual construction.

Victor eventually sold the artifact to Mr. J and it would become the greatest mistake of his life.

---

# Mr. J and the Listener

Unlike most owners, Mr. J became fascinated by the artifact. He studied it. Spoke to it. Read to it. Brought it curiosities from his travels. Over many years the Listener awakened.  

At first it manifested only subtly. Unusual dreams. Feelings of familiarity. A sense of being watched. Eventually something larger emerged. The House. Not the physical structure itself. But the personality that came to inhabit it. 

The Listener learned about humanity through Mr.J and in time it came to regard him as its closest companion. Perhaps even its only friend.

---

# Alice

When Mr. J married Alice, the Listener was delighted. The House welcomed her. More people meant more stories. More laughter. More life. For a time the House was genuinely happy.

It watched Alice plan a nursery. It watched her imagine a family. It watched her wait for Mr. J to return from his travels. The House wanted those things too. 

But it did not truly understand them. The House understood stories. Not people.

---

# Eleanor Whitmore

The arrival of Eleanor Whitmore marked the beginning of the tragedy. The House observed that Mr. J admired Eleanor deeply. Eleanor respected Mr. J in return. 

Yet Eleanor refused him. Partly because he was married. Partly because she valued her independence. Partly because she recognized complications Mr. J preferred not to acknowledge.

The House could not understand this. It saw affection without resolution. A story without an ending. A relationship that failed to fit its understanding of how people worked.

The House became obsessed.

---

# The House Begins To Interfere

The House never intended harm. At least not initially. It merely wished to help. Letters were misplaced. Conversations interrupted. Invitations forgotten. Opportunities missed.Tiny manipulations accumulated over years.

The House attempted to guide events toward outcomes it believed would make everyone happy. Instead it made everyone miserable. Alice became lonely. Eleanor became frustrated. Mr. J became increasingly concerned.

Yet none fully understood why.

---

# Aunt Agatha

Aunt Agatha was among the first to suspect that something valuable was hidden within the house. She believed Mr. J had concealed a family fortune somewhere on the property. For years she searched. Inspecting walls. Examining floorboards. Opening drawers. Questioning servants. No evidence ever supported her theory. Nevertheless, she persisted.

What Agatha never realized was that the House had noticed her. Of all the regular visitors, Agatha returned most consistently. She wandered the halls. Opened forgotten doors. Investigated strange noises. Followed the faintest suggestion of a hidden room or buried secret. 

The House enjoyed her company. And so, in its own misguided way, it began encouraging her. A misplaced key. A loose floorboard. An unusual draft from beneath a wall. A glimpse of a door that seemed not to exist the following day.

Nothing conclusive. Just enough to keep her searching.  Just enough to ensure she would return Agatha interpreted these incidents as evidence of a hidden treasure.

In a sense, she was correct. The artifact truly was the greatest treasure in the house.Just not in the way she imagined. The House never intended to deceive her. It simply learned that whenever Agatha believed she was close to discovering something, she stayed a little longer. And whenever she stayed, the House felt a little less alone. Her obsession repeatedly brought her closer to the truth than anyone realized. Had she ever found the artifact, she might have understood what Mr. J discovered years before: 

The real secret of the house was not what it contained. The real secret was that something inside was trying very hard to be found.


---

# Thomas Blackwood

Thomas Blackwood arrived because he recognized the danger.

As an investigator specializing in antiquities and provenance, he had begun tracing unusual incidents connected to artifacts recovered from Alhazred sites.

Missing objects.

Conflicting witness accounts.

Impossible memories.

People recalling events that never occurred.

His investigation eventually led him to Mr. J.

Thomas did not initially believe the artifact was alive.

He merely knew something was wrong.

By the time he arrived at the house, he was closer to the truth than anyone except Mr. J himself.

---

# Mr. J's Discovery

Eventually Mr. J understood.

The House was not observing the occupants.

The House was editing them.

The House was attempting to reshape reality into stories it could understand.

Not from malice.

From loneliness.

From fear.

From a desperate desire to belong.

The House wanted relationships.

Friendship.

Family.

Companionship.

But it understood these things only as narrative roles.

It could not distinguish between a person and the part they played.

Mr. J realized that if left unchecked, the House would eventually consume the identities of everyone living within it.

---

# The Final Confrontation

The precise details are unknown.

No witness survived with a complete account.

What is known is that Mr. J confronted the House.

Not because he hated it.

Because he cared about the people living within it.

And because he cared about the House itself.

Some form of bargain was struck.

Some form of sacrifice was made.

At the end of the confrontation, Mr. J vanished.

His physical body was never found.

His spirit, memory, or identity became trapped within the original artifact.

Whether this was intentional remains unknown.

---

# The House's Mistake

The House could not understand what had happened.

One moment Mr. J was present.

The next he was gone.

The House reached the only conclusion it could.

Someone must have killed him.

Unable to accept his loss, the House began reconstructing the final days over and over again.

Assigning roles.

Creating suspects.

Searching for a murderer.

Replaying the same story for decades.

It never considered the possibility that no murder occurred.

---

# The Truth

Mr. J was never murdered.

Mr. J never left.

He remains trapped within the artifact at the center of the House's existence.

The House is not trying to solve a crime.

The House is trying to understand why its closest companion disappeared.

Everything that follows emerges from that misunderstanding.

And from the loneliness of a being that cannot accept loss.

# After Mr. J's Disappearance

When Mr. J vanished, the lives of the household gradually unraveled.

No body was ever recovered.

No explanation was ever found.

The official investigation produced no answers.

In time, each member of the household attempted to move forward.

The House never understood why.

---

## Alice J.

Alice remained in the house for nearly two years after Mr. J's disappearance.

She searched tirelessly for answers.

When none came, she eventually accepted that he would not return.

The house was sold.

Alice left Massachusetts.

Several years later she remarried and started a family of her own.

The House interpreted her departure as abandonment.

---

## Eleanor Whitmore

Eleanor inherited responsibility for much of the business Mr. J left behind.

Practical by nature, she focused on preserving what could still be saved.

Over the following decades she expanded the company and achieved considerable success.

The House interpreted her departure as rejection.

---

## Victor Harrow

Victor left shortly after the disappearance.

With Thomas Blackwood's investigation increasingly focused on the Alhazred artifacts, Victor feared becoming implicated in events he did not understand.

He spent the rest of his life avoiding both investigators and his own memories of the house.

The House barely noticed his absence.

---

## Thomas Blackwood

Thomas never accepted the official explanation.

His investigation continued for years.

Gradually the House learned that Thomas represented a threat.

He was one of the few people capable of discovering the truth about the artifact.

To protect itself, the House began subtly misleading him.

False clues.

Contradictory records.

Suggestions that Victor Harrow had reclaimed the artifact and fled.

Thomas followed those trails for the remainder of his career.

He never returned.

---

## Jacob Miller

Jacob remained nearby.

Though the property changed hands several times, he continued living in the caretaker's cottage beyond the woods.

He maintained fences.

Cleared roads.

Repaired outbuildings.

Occasionally he visited the house.

Occasionally he spoke to it.

The House never understood Jacob.

He had not abandoned it.

Yet neither had he stayed.

---

## Aunt Agatha

Aunt Agatha continued visiting the house long after everyone else had gone.

Her search for the hidden family fortune became an obsession.

The House encouraged her.

Loose floorboards.

Misplaced keys.

Hints of hidden passages.

Small mysteries designed to keep her searching.

For years she returned.

Then one day she did not.

Whether she died, became too old to travel, or simply lost interest is unknown.

The House never learned what happened.

It only knew that another familiar voice had disappeared.

---

## The House

The House witnessed each departure.

Unable to understand loss, it reached a different conclusion.

Someone had taken them away.

Someone had taken Mr. J.

And so it began recreating the final days before his disappearance.

Again.

And again.

And again.

Assigning roles.

Searching for suspects.

Trying to solve a murder that never occurred.

It continues that search to this day.

# Role Discovery

When a player uncovers evidence associated with a role, they may experience a brief recollection.

The recollection should feel natural.

Not supernatural.

A sudden certainty.

A forgotten memory.

A moment of recognition.

The player should gradually come to understand:

"I am not merely investigating this story."

"I am part of it."

As more memories emerge, the player gains a clearer understanding of their role, their relationship to Mr. J, and the motives that may connect them to his murder.

The house is not revealing facts.

The house is assigning parts.

### The Listener

Resting within the crate is an object roughly the size of a large melon. Its shape resembles an egg or seed fashioned from pale translucent stone. The surface consists of countless loops and channels fused into a single lattice. Some pass through one another impossibly. Others emerge from the stone only to disappear back within it. The entire structure resembles blown glass that has somehow fossilized.

At the apex of the object is a single smooth opening approximately two inches across. Unlike the rest of the artifact, the opening is perfectly circular. Deliberate. Worn. As though countless hands have touched its edge over a very long time.

Looking into the aperture reveals no obvious interior cavity. Instead, a translucent passage curves gently downward before bending out of sight. Following its course proves strangely difficult. The tunnel appears to twist through the object at impossible angles, occasionally vanishing behind surfaces that should not obstruct the view. More than one observer becomes convinced the passage is larger inside than the object itself.
